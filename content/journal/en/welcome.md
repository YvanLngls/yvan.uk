# Intro

 > *« The good thing about computers is that they do what you tell them to do. The bad thing is that they do exactly what you tell them to do.»*
 >
 > *Ted Nelson  -  1974*

As computing has reached a state of ubiquity in our lives, it has ceased to be merely a technical discipline to become a mirror of our society, and perhaps even of our own humanity. While it’s impossible to grasp all of its complexity, this space aims to document my personal relationship with technology through my research and experiments.

This first post lays the foundation for the project. We’ll first explore its origins and roots, then define the goals that drive it. Finally, we’ll outline the first milestones you can follow here in the coming time.

# Genesis

For years, my technical horizon was confined by dormitory walls and a 9m² student housing room. Hardly ideal conditions to set up a self-hosted homelab cluster. But now, I’ve finally been able to start building my own little lab. Using my brother’s old laptop and a second-hand PC, I’ve begun experimenting with a small personal infrastructure. Right now, not much is fully functional, and the setup is powered off most of the time. I’m just starting to lay down the pipelines for what I want to use in future projects. Like many enthusiasts, I’ve started far more projects than I’ve ever finished. That’s exactly why I felt the need for a dedicated space to honor them. To catalog them, and to keep them from fading into oblivion. 

# Manifesto

So, welcome to my digital garden. Here, I’ll share my projects, my ideas, and my failures too. I plan to write about the things I’m building, the technologies that catch my eyes, and I’ll also document projects I might never actually complete. This site will also serve as a testing ground where I might add new sections on a whim. On top of that, I might occasionally share my thoughts on broader topics, like the rise of artificial intelligence in our daily lives and its impact, or other subjects that cross my mind. Putting thoughts into writing helps clarify them, it’s an exercise I don’t practice enough, and this feels like the perfect opportunity to start.

A quick disclaimer: I make mistakes, and I don’t hold the truth. I can change my mind, and I’m quick to admit when I’m wrong. So, it’s entirely possible that what I write here will be partially inaccurate or outdated. Always stay critical. I’m fully aware of how much I still don’t know, and I invite you to form your own opinions as you read.

What this site is not:
 - It’s not official documentation or a definitive guide to best practices
 - It’s not a commercial showcase
 - It’s not a tech news site. If a topic fascinates me three months after everyone else has moved on, I’ll still write about it

Ultimately, this site is my little island in the vast ocean of the internet. Here, I’ll build in public, share my wins and my stumbles, my discoveries, and the projects I support. I invite you to pull up a chair and linger for a while. I hope you’ll find these pages a refreshing change of pace. 

# Roadmap

The next milestones I’m aiming for are writing my first posts about the technologies that interest me, the challenges I’m facing, and the projects that keep me motivated.

First up, I plan to dive into self-hosting. I’ll write about my k3s homelab: what I’ve done so far, what’s already working, the roadblocks I’m hitting, and the goals I have for the services running on my personal infrastructure. I might also touch on new tools that catch my eye, but overall, I’m just excited about this project because it lets me build an infrastructure from the ground up. I’ll probably focus especially on the architecture phase, which is by far the most fun part.

Next, I’ll talk about side projects. Apps I’m trying to build in my spare time, robotics that I’m now looking to get into, and the slow-but-steady start of some DIY home automation.

Finally, I’ll likely dedicate a portion of this log to my reflections on keeping a critical eye on the world around us, especially regarding disruptive technologies (AI, quantum computing, etc.) and the major players shaping them, which make digital sovereignty one of the defining challenges of our time.

# Conclusion

This blog isn’t a destination, but a starting point. It doesn’t claim to be exhaustive or flawless—just honest. It’s a logbook where I learn out loud, where unfinished projects have their place, and where mistakes aren’t dead ends, but necessary steps toward understanding.

Thanks for reading these first few lines. Whether you’re a developer, a tinkerer, a curious mind, or just passing through, your perspective, your feedback, or even your disagreements are more than welcome. A digital garden only comes alive when people wander through it.

Happy reading 🫡
